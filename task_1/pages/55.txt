<p><b>Hidetaka Miyazaki</b><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>: <span lang="ja">宮崎 英高</span>, <a href="/wiki/Hepburn_romanization" title="Hepburn romanization">Hepburn</a>: <span title="Hepburn transliteration"><i lang="ja-Latn">Miyazaki Hidetaka</i></span>; born September 19, 1974)</span> is a Japanese <a href="/wiki/Creative_director" title="Creative director">video game director</a>, designer, writer, and president of the game developer <a href="/wiki/FromSoftware" title="FromSoftware">FromSoftware</a>. He joined the company in 2004 and was a designer for the <i><a href="/wiki/Armored_Core" title="Armored Core">Armored Core</a></i> series before receiving wider recognition for creating the <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Souls" title="Dark Souls">Dark Souls</a></i> series. Miyazaki was promoted to <a href="/wiki/Company_president" class="mw-redirect" title="Company president">company president</a> in 2014 and also serves as its <a href="/wiki/Representative_director_(Japan)" title="Representative director (Japan)">representative director</a>. Other similar games he has directed include <i><a href="/wiki/Demon%27s_Souls" title="Demon&#39;s Souls">Demon's Souls</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bloodborne" title="Bloodborne">Bloodborne</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sekiro" class="mw-redirect" title="Sekiro">Sekiro</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Elden_Ring" title="Elden Ring">Elden Ring</a></i>.
</p><p>Miyazaki's influences range across the works of various novelists, <a href="/wiki/Manga_artists" class="mw-redirect" title="Manga artists">manga artists</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Game_designers" class="mw-redirect" title="Game designers">game designers</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Fumito_Ueda" title="Fumito Ueda">Fumito Ueda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yuji_Horii" title="Yuji Horii">Yuji Horii</a>. His games often invoke the use of <a href="/wiki/Game_balance#Difficulty_level" title="Game balance">high difficulty</a> and narratives presented largely through <a href="/wiki/Flavor_text" title="Flavor text">flavor text</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visual_storytelling" class="mw-redirect" title="Visual storytelling">environmental cues</a> as opposed to dialogue. Regarded as an <a href="/wiki/Auteur" title="Auteur">auteur</a> of video games, Miyazaki's works have been cited as among the <a href="/wiki/List_of_video_games_considered_the_best" class="mw-redirect" title="List of video games considered the best">greatest in the medium</a>, leading to the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Soulslike" title="Soulslike">Soulslike</a> subgenre.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Career">Career</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hidetaka_Miyazaki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Miyazaki was born on September 19, 1974,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and grew up in <a href="/wiki/Shizuoka,_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="Shizuoka, Japan">Shizuoka, Japan</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-guardian--interview&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian--interview-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He later attended <a href="/wiki/Keio_University" title="Keio University">Keio University</a> and graduated with a degree in <a href="/wiki/Social_science" title="Social science">social science</a>, later getting a job as an <a href="/wiki/Account_manager" title="Account manager">account manager</a> for the US-based <a href="/wiki/Oracle_Corporation" title="Oracle Corporation">Oracle Corporation</a> to pay for his sister's college tuition fees.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NewYorker&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorker-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-andriasang&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-andriasang-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Upon a friend's recommendation, Miyazaki played the 2001 video game <i><a href="/wiki/Ico" title="Ico">Ico</a></i>, which caused him to want a career change as a <a href="/wiki/Game_designer" class="mw-redirect" title="Game designer">game designer</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-guardian--interview&#95;3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian--interview-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Miyazaki found that few game companies would employ him at age 29 with no experience working in the industry, with one of the few being <a href="/wiki/FromSoftware" title="FromSoftware">FromSoftware</a>. He began working there as a planner on <i><a href="/wiki/Armored_Core:_Last_Raven" title="Armored Core: Last Raven">Armored Core: Last Raven</a></i> in 2004, joining the game's development midway through.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-guardian--interview&#95;3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian--interview-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-andriasang&#95;5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-andriasang-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He later directed <i><a href="/wiki/Armored_Core_4" title="Armored Core 4">Armored Core 4</a></i> and its direct sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Armored_Core:_For_Answer" title="Armored Core: For Answer">Armored Core: For Answer</a></i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-andriasang&#95;5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-andriasang-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Upon learning about what later became <i><a href="/wiki/Demon%27s_Souls" title="Demon&#39;s Souls">Demon's Souls</a></i>, Miyazaki became excited at the prospect of a fantasy <a href="/wiki/Action_role-playing_game" title="Action role-playing game">action role-playing game</a> and offered to help.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-guardian--interview&#95;3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian--interview-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The project, up until he was assigned to it, was considered a failure by the company. He believed the company's outlook on the game allowed him to take full control of the project as any further failed ideas would not hurt it.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-guardian--interview&#95;3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian--interview-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Although the game was received negatively at the 2009 <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Game_Show" title="Tokyo Game Show">Tokyo Game Show</a> and sold far under expectations upon release, it began to pick up after a few months and soon found publishers willing to release the game outside of Japan.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-guardian--interview&#95;3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian--interview-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> After the success of the game's <a href="/wiki/Spiritual_successor" title="Spiritual successor">spiritual successor</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Souls_(video_game)" title="Dark Souls (video game)">Dark Souls</a></i>, released in 2011, Miyazaki was promoted to the position of company president in May 2014.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It was considered unprecedented for a person to change careers in Japan and become company president within 10 years.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-guardian--interview&#95;3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian--interview-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, <a href="/wiki/Sony_Computer_Entertainment" class="mw-redirect" title="Sony Computer Entertainment">Sony Computer Entertainment</a> approached FromSoftware concerning cooperative development on a new game. Miyazaki asked about the possibility of developing a game for <a href="/wiki/Eighth-generation_consoles" class="mw-redirect" title="Eighth-generation consoles">eighth-generation consoles</a>, and the concept of <i><a href="/wiki/Bloodborne" title="Bloodborne">Bloodborne</a></i> developed from there. Even though there were no story or setting connections to FromSoftware's previous games, Miyazaki said that it carried the "DNA" of <i>Demon's Souls</i> and its specific <a href="/wiki/Level_design" class="mw-redirect" title="Level design">level design</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-IGNinterview&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGNinterview-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Development ran parallel to that of <i>Dark Souls II</i>, which Miyazaki supervised only as he was unable to direct both games simultaneously.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Miyazaki returned to the <i>Dark Souls</i> series as the lead director on <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Souls_III" title="Dark Souls III">Dark Souls III</a></i>, which released early the following year.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> After its release, he announced his intentions to personally stop development on the <i>Dark Souls</i> series.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> His next project was the 2018 <a href="/wiki/Virtual_reality" title="Virtual reality">virtual reality</a> game <i><a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9racin%C3%A9" title="Déraciné">Déraciné</a></i> and the 2019 action-adventure game <i><a href="/wiki/Sekiro:_Shadows_Die_Twice" title="Sekiro: Shadows Die Twice">Sekiro: Shadows Die Twice</a></i>, with the latter winning several awards.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Miyazaki directed the 2022 release <i><a href="/wiki/Elden_Ring" title="Elden Ring">Elden Ring</a></i>, approaching American fantasy writer <a href="/wiki/George_R._R._Martin" title="George R. R. Martin">George R. R. Martin</a> to provide its worldbuilding.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The game sold more than <span class="nowrap">25 million</span> copies and has been cited as among the <a href="/wiki/Greatest_video_games_of_all_time" class="mw-redirect" title="Greatest video games of all time">greatest video games of all time</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Miyazaki later served as the initial director of <i><a href="/wiki/Armored_Core_VI:_Fires_of_Rubicon" title="Armored Core VI: Fires of Rubicon">Armored Core VI: Fires of Rubicon</a></i> (2023) before Masaru Yamamura assumed the role.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

One such example is <a href="/wiki/Anor_Londo" title="Anor Londo">Anor Londo</a>, a central location in <i>Dark Souls</i>, with parts being modeled after the <a href="/wiki/Milan_Cathedral" title="Milan Cathedral">Milan Cathedral</a> in Italy (<i>top</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau_de_Chambord" title="Château de Chambord">Château de Chambord</a> in France (<i>bottom</i>).</div></div></div></div>
<p>Miyazaki was an avid reader as a child despite his parents being unable to afford him many books.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-guardian--interview&#95;3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian--interview-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He frequently borrowed from his local library, including English language <a href="/wiki/Fantasy_literature" title="Fantasy literature">fantasy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> that he did not fully understand, allowing his imagination to fill in the blanks by using the accompanying illustrations.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NewYorker&#95;4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorker-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-guardian--interview&#95;3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian--interview-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He would later cite that as a major influence on his design philosophy. Miyazaki also played <a href="/wiki/Gamebook" title="Gamebook">gamebooks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tabletop_game" title="Tabletop game">tabletop games</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Steve_Jackson%27s_Sorcery!" title="Steve Jackson&#39;s Sorcery!">Steve Jackson's Sorcery!</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/RuneQuest" title="RuneQuest">RuneQuest</a></i> as his parents restricted him from playing video games until he was old enough to attend university.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-IGNinterview&#95;8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGNinterview-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-RollingStone&#95;21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RollingStone-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Once exposed to video games, Miyazaki became a fan of <i>Ico</i>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-guardian--interview&#95;3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian--interview-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> the early <i><a href="/wiki/Dragon_Quest" title="Dragon Quest">Dragon Quest</a></i> games,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-edge&#95;23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edge-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Legend_of_Zelda" title="The Legend of Zelda">The Legend of Zelda</a></i>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-edge&#95;23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edge-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-RollingStone&#95;21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RollingStone-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and the <i><a href="/wiki/King%27s_Field" title="King&#39;s Field">King's Field</a></i> series, all of which he cited as influences.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He is also a fan of <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Berserk_(manga)" title="Berserk (manga)">Berserk</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Saint_Seiya" title="Saint Seiya">Saint Seiya</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/JoJo%27s_Bizarre_Adventure" title="JoJo&#39;s Bizarre Adventure">JoJo's Bizarre Adventure</a></i>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Devilman" title="Devilman">Devilman</a></i>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-RollingStone&#95;21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RollingStone-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> as well as the literature of <a href="/wiki/H._P._Lovecraft" title="H. P. Lovecraft">H. P. Lovecraft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bram_Stoker" title="Bram Stoker">Bram Stoker</a>, and George R. R. Martin.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-RollingStone&#95;21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RollingStone-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Miyazaki is also inspired by <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>, especially of European origin, and often uses it in his games as a way of <a href="/wiki/Visual_narrative" title="Visual narrative">environmental storytelling</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-otsuka&#95;29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-otsuka-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Wired&#95;30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wired-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">Psychology</a>  and <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a> have also influenced his design choices.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-IGNinterview&#95;8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IGNinterview-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>As the lead creative director on a project, Miyazaki usually writes the majority of the story, dialogue, and text, while having the final say on character, monster, and level designs. The multiplayer mechanics of the <i>Souls</i> series were inspired by his own personal experience of driving up a snowy road as cars ahead began slipping back and were pushed uphill by other people in the area. As Miyazaki was unable to give his thanks before they left the area, he wondered whether the last person in the line had made it to their destination as he was unlikely to ever meet them again. This gave birth to the series' multiplayer systems, with it attempting to emulate that same sense of silent cooperation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-SoulsSurvivor&#95;33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SoulsSurvivor-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Miyazaki stated that the difficulty of his games have no intention of being that way compared to others. Rather, the difficulty was a part of the process that gives players a sense of accomplishment by "overcoming tremendous odds", while also incentivizing players to experiment more with <a href="/wiki/Character_builds" class="mw-redirect" title="Character builds">character builds</a> and weapons.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Wired&#95;30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wired-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He stated that death in his games is used as a <a href="/wiki/Trial_and_error" title="Trial and error">trial and error</a> learning tool, adding that the idea became accepted by the public following the success of <i>Demon's Souls</i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Miyazaki stated that he does not dislike direct storytelling but prefers players to interpret it for themselves, as they get more value from it when they find out hints of plot from items or side-characters.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Wired&#95;30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wired-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Many journalists and critics have cited him an <a href="/wiki/Auteur" title="Auteur">auteur</a> of video games, noting his influential work in <i>Dark Souls</i> and its related games.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Awards_and_honors">Awards and honors</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hidetaka_Miyazaki&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Awards and honors"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Miyazaki was awarded the Lifetime Achievement Award at the <a href="/wiki/2018_Golden_Joystick_Awards" title="2018 Golden Joystick Awards">2018 Golden Joystick Awards</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The award was presented to him by <a href="/wiki/Ian_Livingstone" title="Ian Livingstone">Ian Livingstone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Jackson_(British_game_designer)" title="Steve Jackson (British game designer)">Steve Jackson</a>, two of his inspirations.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2022, he was the recipient of the Special Award at the <a href="/wiki/CEDEC_Awards" title="CEDEC Awards">CEDEC Awards</a> and the Minister of Economy, Trade, and Industry Award at the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Game_Awards" title="Japan Game Awards">Japan Game Awards</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 2023, <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> listed Miyazaki as one of the <a href="/wiki/100_Most_Influential_People" class="mw-redirect" title="100 Most Influential People">100 Most Influential People</a> of the year, making him the second game developer to be featured on the list after <a href="/wiki/Shigeru_Miyamoto" title="Shigeru Miyamoto">Shigeru Miyamoto</a> in 2007.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

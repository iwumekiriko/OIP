<p><b>Herbert Collins</b> (1885–1975) was a British architect, born in <a href="/wiki/Edmonton,_London" title="Edmonton, London">Edmonton, London</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> He designed some of the finest suburban developments in the city of <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a> in the 1920s and 1930s.
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Life">Life</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Collins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Collins designed houses in Southampton from 1922,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-echo&#95;heritage&#95;2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-echo_heritage-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> became a director of the <a href="/wiki/Welwyn_Garden_City" title="Welwyn Garden City">Welwyn Garden City</a> Company in 1924<sup id="cite&#95;ref-highfieldremembered&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-highfieldremembered-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Purdom&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdom-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and co-founded the <a href="/wiki/Swaythling" title="Swaythling">Swaythling</a> <a href="/wiki/Housing_society" class="mw-redirect" title="Housing society">Housing society</a> on 26 November 1925.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-echo&#95;heritage&#95;2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-echo_heritage-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Collins, along with his cofounders, accountant and civic leader Fred Woolley (the society's first chairman) and <a href="/wiki/Bursledon" title="Bursledon">Bursledon</a> <a href="/wiki/Brickworks" title="Brickworks">brickworks</a> director Claude Ashby, put up £200 worth of shares.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-echo&#95;heritage&#95;2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-echo_heritage-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Collins' father, William, loaned the fledgling company £14,000.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-echo&#95;heritage&#95;2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-echo_heritage-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Collins lived at 38 Brookvale Road in <a href="/wiki/Highfield,_Southampton" title="Highfield, Southampton">Highfield, Southampton</a> from 1930 to 1973, and a commemorative <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a> <a href="/wiki/Blue_plaque" title="Blue plaque">blue plaque</a> was installed there in July 2004.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-blueplaque&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blueplaque-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> During his time living there, Collins was responsible for the design of <a href="/wiki/Swaythling_Methodist_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Swaythling Methodist Church">Swaythling Methodist Church</a> in Burgess Road, built in 1932.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In an effort to contribute to the post-war housing efforts, Collins submitted a suggestion to the July 1945 edition of the journal <i>Architectural Design and Construction</i> regarding constructing <a href="/wiki/Bungalow" title="Bungalow">bungalows</a> from <a href="/wiki/Rammed_earth" title="Rammed earth">rammed earth</a> combined with a small quantity of cement; his suggestion was repeated in <i>The Architects' Journal</i> in 1946.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 1957, Collins retired as a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Institute_of_British_Architects" title="Royal Institute of British Architects">Royal Institute of British Architects</a> and his professional partnership with J. Normal Calton was dissolved by mutual consent; the pair had been trading as Collins &amp; Calton.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-RIBA&#95;1957&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIBA_1957-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> At this time, Collins was living at 32 Carlton Crescent in Southampton.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-RIBA&#95;1957&#95;8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIBA_1957-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Collins' retirement, however, did not mean an end to his interest in architecture and town planning, and in January 1962 a letter from Collins, sent from his Carlton Crescent address, was published in the <i>Journal of the Town Planning Institute</i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Work">Work</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Collins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Collins' housing estates have a distinctive style, usually with rows of terraced houses set around wide areas of greenery.
</p><p>Collins also made plans for a <a href="/wiki/Garden_city_movement" title="Garden city movement">garden city</a> around <a href="/wiki/Marchwood" title="Marchwood">Marchwood</a>, but these proposals were unrealised.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-echo&#95;heritage&#95;2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-echo_heritage-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-highfieldremembered&#95;3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-highfieldremembered-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Rookfield_Estate">Rookfield Estate</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Collins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rookfield Estate"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Cascade_Avenue,_N10_-_geograph.org.uk_-_472961.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Cascade_Avenue%2C_N10_-_geograph.org.uk_-_472961.jpg/250px-Cascade_Avenue%2C_N10_-_geograph.org.uk_-_472961.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="166" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Cascade_Avenue%2C_N10_-_geograph.org.uk_-_472961.jpg/500px-Cascade_Avenue%2C_N10_-_geograph.org.uk_-_472961.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="426" /></a><figcaption>Cascade Avenue</figcaption></figure>
<p>The Rookfield estate (also known as Rookfield Garden Village<sup id="cite&#95;ref-vch&#95;10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vch-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or Rookfield Garden Estate<sup id="cite&#95;ref-rookfield-dot-com&#95;11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rookfield-dot-com-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>) is situated near <a href="/wiki/Muswell_Hill" title="Muswell Hill">Muswell Hill</a> in the <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Haringey" title="London Borough of Haringey">London Borough of Haringey</a>. It comprises Cascade Avenue, Rookfield Avenue and adjacent roads.
</p><p>The Rookfield Estate <a href="/wiki/Conservation_area_(United_Kingdom)" title="Conservation area (United Kingdom)">conservation area</a> was designated on 26 March 1976 and an <a href="/wiki/Article_4_direction" title="Article 4 direction">Article 4 direction</a> has been in place for the estate since 1978.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Collins' father William Jeffries Collins began the development of the Rookfield estate some time before 1910<sup id="cite&#95;ref-vch&#95;10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vch-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> on land purchased in 1899.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-rookfield-dot-com&#95;11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rookfield-dot-com-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> When William Jeffries Collins moved his business to Southampton in 1911, his sons Herbert and William Brannan Collins took over the development. The estate was completed in 1936.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Uplands_Estate">Uplands Estate</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Collins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Uplands Estate"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The Uplands estate, in <a href="/wiki/Highfield,_Southampton" title="Highfield, Southampton">Highfield, Southampton</a> was built between 1922 and 1936. It was the first development undertaken by Herbert Collins in Southampton. The Uplands Estate (Highfield) conservation area was designated in January 1986.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Bassett_Green_Estate">Bassett Green Estate</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Collins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bassett Green Estate"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bassett_Green" title="Bassett Green">Bassett Green</a> estate, which comprises Ethelburt Avenue and parts of Bassett Green Road and Leaside Way, was developed on land bought by William Jeffries Collins in 1925. The Ethelburt Avenue (Bassett Green Estate) conservation area was designated by <a href="/wiki/Southampton_City_Council" title="Southampton City Council">Southampton City Council</a> in September 1988.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Swaythling_Methodist_Church">Swaythling Methodist Church</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Collins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Swaythling Methodist Church"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/286,_Southampton" title="286, Southampton">Swaythling Methodist Church</a> was built in 1932 in a <a href="/wiki/Georgian_architecture#Post-Georgian_developments" title="Georgian architecture">neo-Georgian</a> style. It is <a href="/wiki/Listed_building#Categories_of_listed_building" title="Listed building">Grade II listed</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Climping_Village_Hall">Climping Village Hall</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Collins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climping Village Hall"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The village hall in <a href="/wiki/Climping" title="Climping">Climping</a> West Sussex was designed by Herbert Collins.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-ReferenceA&#95;17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Orchards_Way">Orchards Way</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Collins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Orchards Way"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:West_End_Fire_Station.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/West_End_Fire_Station.jpg/250px-West_End_Fire_Station.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="150" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/West_End_Fire_Station.jpg/500px-West_End_Fire_Station.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a><figcaption>The Collins-designed fire station in <a href="/wiki/West_End,_Hampshire" title="West End, Hampshire">West End, Hampshire</a>, now a local history museum</figcaption></figure>
<p>The Orchards Way estate is situated in <a href="/wiki/West_End,_Hampshire" title="West End, Hampshire">West End, Hampshire</a>. Designed in 1936 for the Hampshire Rural Cottage Improvement Society, it originally comprised 32 cottages, grouped in short terraces, two shops and a fire station and was more rurally situated than Collins' earlier developments. The Orchards Way conservation area was designated by <a href="/wiki/Borough_of_Eastleigh" title="Borough of Eastleigh">Eastleigh Borough Council</a> in 1999.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Chandler's_Ford_United_Reformed_Church"><span id="Chandler.27s_Ford_United_Reformed_Church"></span>Chandler's Ford United Reformed Church</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Collins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Chandler&#039;s Ford United Reformed Church"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Originally Chandler's Ford Congregational Church, the building was designed by Collins in 1929.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-ReferenceA&#95;17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> It has been extended and enlarged since then but the significant elements of the original style retained as shown on the church's website.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

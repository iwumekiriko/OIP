<p><b>Gregory Allen Reeves</b> (born 1949 or 1950)<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-rs&#95;2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rs-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> is an American <a href="/wiki/Bass_guitar" title="Bass guitar">bass guitarist</a>. He is best known for playing bass on <a href="/wiki/Crosby,_Stills,_Nash_%26_Young" title="Crosby, Stills, Nash &amp; Young">Crosby, Stills, Nash &amp; Young</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9j%C3%A0_Vu_(Crosby,_Stills,_Nash_%26_Young_album)" title="Déjà Vu (Crosby, Stills, Nash &amp; Young album)">Déjà Vu</a></i> (1970).
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Early_life">Early life</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greg_Reeves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Reeves grew up in <a href="/wiki/Warren,_Ohio" title="Warren, Ohio">Warren, Ohio</a>, and graduated from Warren Western Reserve High School in 1968.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In an interview, bandmate <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Taylor_(drummer)" title="Dallas Taylor (drummer)">Dallas Taylor</a> spoke of a rumor that Reeves was as young as 15 years old when he joined CSNY.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-taylor&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> While Reeves acknowledged having a <a href="/wiki/Identity_document_forgery" title="Identity document forgery">fake ID</a> and being "very young" while with CSNY,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-waysite1&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waysite1-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> stated his age as 19 in a December 1969 article.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-rs&#95;2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rs-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Career">Career</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greg_Reeves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Motown">Motown</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greg_Reeves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Motown"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Reeves had been scouted as a session musician at the age of 12 by <a href="/wiki/Motown_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Motown Records">Motown Records</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chess_Records" title="Chess Records">Chess Records</a>, although his mother "would not hear of" him playing professionally at that age.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-waysite1&#95;5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waysite1-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-robesonian&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-robesonian-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> By 1968, however, Reeves was employed as a session bassist at Motown. During the recording session for <a href="/wiki/The_Temptations" title="The Temptations">The Temptations</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Cloud_Nine_(The_Temptations_song)" title="Cloud Nine (The Temptations song)">Cloud Nine</a>", Reeves was apprehensive about his bass playing and switched to <a href="/wiki/Tambourine" title="Tambourine">tambourine</a> at the behest of producer <a href="/wiki/Norman_Whitfield" title="Norman Whitfield">Norman Whitfield</a>, his recruiter and main benefactor at Motown.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-waysite1&#95;5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waysite1-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-csn94&#95;7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-csn94-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed. (June 2021)">full citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> During this period, Reeves was also mentored by other Motown musicians, including <a href="/wiki/Rick_James" title="Rick James">Rick James</a> (who concurrently played alongside Reeves in Salt 'n' Pepa, a <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> rock group formed from the remnants of <a href="/wiki/Merryweather_(band)" title="Merryweather (band)">Merryweather</a>) and <a href="/wiki/James_Jamerson" title="James Jamerson">James Jamerson</a>. Reeves stated that his most notable performance for the company was the bass part (overdubbed in Los Angeles) of "<a href="/wiki/No_Matter_What_Sign_You_Are" title="No Matter What Sign You Are">No Matter What Sign You Are</a>", the final song recorded by <a href="/wiki/Diana_Ross" title="Diana Ross">Diana Ross</a> with <a href="/wiki/The_Supremes" title="The Supremes">The Supremes</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-waysite1&#95;5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waysite1-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-shakey&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shakey-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Work_with_CSNY">Work with CSNY</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greg_Reeves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Work with CSNY"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Reeves recorded and toured with <a href="/wiki/Crosby,_Stills,_Nash_%26_Young" title="Crosby, Stills, Nash &amp; Young">Crosby, Stills, Nash &amp; Young</a> from August 1969 to January 1970 and is credited on the cover of their 1970 <i><a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9j%C3%A0_Vu_(Crosby,_Stills,_Nash_%26_Young_album)" title="Déjà Vu (Crosby, Stills, Nash &amp; Young album)">Déjà Vu</a></i> album; he appears with the group in the concert documentary <i><a href="/wiki/Celebration_at_Big_Sur" title="Celebration at Big Sur">Celebration at Big Sur</a></i> (filmed in September 1969) and in contemporaneous television appearances on <i><a href="/wiki/This_Is_Tom_Jones" title="This Is Tom Jones">This Is Tom Jones</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Music_Scene_(TV_series)" title="The Music Scene (TV series)">The Music Scene</a></i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Reeves was living with mentor Rick James in Los Angeles when he was recruited for CSNY:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1244412712">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;margin-top:0}@media(min-width:500px){.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{padding-left:1.6em}}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>David [Crosby] and Graham [Nash] drove up to the apartment where we lived on Olive Drive at the time ... from the pool I saw them knock on the door, and Rick yelled for me. They asked Rick James who I was and Rick came, got me and brought me out front to the limousine that Graham Nash and David Crosby were in and asked me in front of them, would I mind going with them to jam. I asked Rick James "Aren't you coming, too?" He said "They want you to come alone." I went back to the house, grabbed my <a href="/wiki/Fender_Precision_Bass" title="Fender Precision Bass">Fender Precision Bass</a> guitar and Gibson <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_guitar" title="Acoustic guitar">acoustic guitar</a>, climbed into the limousine and never came back to Rick's house again. They would not let me go. From the first time jamming with me, they would not let me leave ... to this day I don't how Graham and David knew where Rick and I lived.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-waysite1&#95;5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waysite1-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>In consultation with other band members, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Stills" title="Stephen Stills">Stephen Stills</a> fired Reeves from the group in April 1970 "because [Reeves] suddenly decided he was an <a href="/wiki/Apache" title="Apache">Apache</a> <a href="/wiki/Witch_doctor" title="Witch doctor">witch doctor</a>."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-csn124&#95;12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-csn124-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Stills further opined that Reeves "freaked too much on the bass and no one could keep up because [he] did not play one rhythm the same. He could play bass imaginatively, but he has to be predictable as well", while Reeves also wanted to sing some of his songs during CSN&amp;Y shows, which Stills thought was "ludicrous, only because the songs weren't great. We'll sing any song if it's great, but not just because it happens to be written by our bass player."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-waysite1&#95;5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waysite1-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-csn124&#95;12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-csn124-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Dallas Taylor (who seldom fraternized with Reeves and was dismissed from the group a month later at the instigation of Neil Young) later noted that while Reeves and Stills did not get along, Reeves and Young were good friends. Following Reeves's termination, Young defied his bandmates and continued to collaborate with Reeves.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-taylor&#95;4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-waysite1&#95;5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waysite1-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Reeves has alleged that the "bass hook" of one of his songs was appropriated by Stills for the main riff of "<a href="/wiki/Carry_On_(CSN%26Y_Song)" class="mw-redirect" title="Carry On (CSN&amp;Y Song)">Carry On</a>". Heeding the advice of James, he demurred from pursuing the issue after receiving a profit point on <i>Déjà Vu</i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-waysite1&#95;5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waysite1-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Although he has conceded that "[the band] thought I was trying to put spells on them" due to his strong interest in <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> <a href="/wiki/Shamanism" title="Shamanism">shamanism</a>, Reeves ultimately ascribed his termination to "Stills [having] a problem with himself, just dealing with himself."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-waysite1&#95;5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waysite1-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Post_CSNY">Post CSNY</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greg_Reeves&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post CSNY"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Reeves's song "I Got Your Number" has been recorded by artists including <a href="/wiki/Tom_Jones_(singer)" title="Tom Jones (singer)">Tom Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boz_Scaggs" title="Boz Scaggs">Boz Scaggs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Bristol" title="Johnny Bristol">Johnny Bristol</a>. Reeves was incarcerated in Mexico from September 1977 to April 1978, saying that he "spent time ... with the Mexican president's nephew as a political prisoner, because we had tried to smuggle marijuana across the border ... Neil Young sent me money (via <a href="/wiki/Western_Union" title="Western Union">Western Union</a>) to pay the Mexican police (<a href="/wiki/Federal_Judicial_Police" title="Federal Judicial Police">Judicial Government Police</a>) for my freedom."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-taylor&#95;4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taylor-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-waysite1&#95;5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waysite1-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>His 1978 $1 million lawsuit pertaining to unpaid CSN&amp;Y royalties was eventually settled amicably.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-waysite1&#95;5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-waysite1-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the aftermath of his tenure with the group, Reeves nevertheless contributed in earnest to several projects, including most of Young's <i><a href="/wiki/After_the_Gold_Rush" title="After the Gold Rush">After the Gold Rush</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Crosby_%26_Nash" title="Crosby &amp; Nash">Crosby &amp; Nash</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Graham_Nash_David_Crosby" title="Graham Nash David Crosby">eponymous debut</a> ("Immigration Man"; 1972), <a href="/wiki/Dave_Mason" title="Dave Mason">Dave Mason</a>'s <i>It's Like You Never Left</i> (1973), an unreleased version of "Tonight's the Night" recorded by Young in early 1974, and indeterminate recordings with <a href="/wiki/George_Clinton_(funk_musician)" title="George Clinton (funk musician)">George Clinton</a> following the temporary dissolution of <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Funkadelic" class="mw-redirect" title="Parliament Funkadelic">Parliament Funkadelic</a> in the early 1980s.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-am-credits&#95;13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-am-credits-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-am-atgr&#95;14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-am-atgr-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nils_Lofgren" title="Nils Lofgren">Nils Lofgren</a> said that during one of the sessions for <i>After the Gold Rush</i>, Reeves appeared covered head to toe in gold paint; Young explained the bassist was "doin' his Indian thing."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-shakey&#95;8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shakey-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Reeves was briefly interviewed by author <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_McDonough" title="Jimmy McDonough">Jimmy McDonough</a> for <i>Shakey</i>, a 2003 biography of Neil Young.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-shakey&#95;8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shakey-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Both Young and Lofgren continue to laud Reeves's playing style, noting his versatility and ability to move fluidly between simple and complex bass lines.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-shakey&#95;8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shakey-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
